A guide to reading Panini's grammar the way Panini intended

1. Introduction

The Ashtadhyayi is not a text you read passively — it is a text you unfold, sutra by sutra, each one carrying forward into the next. A prior sutra reveals the meaning of later sutras in a way that, once seen, is never forgotten for a lifetime.

Students who learn to derive the meaning (वृत्ति) of each sutra on their own, never forget in lifetime — this is Panini's method of teaching. The lifeless, rote approach — memorizing meanings without understanding the mechanism — drains the joy and the life out of the subject and must be set aside.

Once you can work through the सूत्रार्थ (meaning of the sutras) this way, you will be able to read texts like the Bhattikavya and other शास्त्र independently, discovering the meaning through your own understanding. This is the science of the Ashtadhyayi.

2. The Method: Building the वृत्ति

A वृत्ति is a full syntactic expansion of a sutra — gathering all the necessary words that belong to it (from the sutra itself, from governing अधिकार sutras, and from words that carry over from above) and arranging them into a coherent sentence.

There are two main techniques:

2.1 Deriving the वृत्ति Using अधिकार (Governing) Sutras

Consider वर्तमाने लट् (3.2.123).

पदच्छेद (word-analysis): वर्तमाने ७/१, लट् १/१ — a two-word sutra. Into it come two adhikara (governing) sutras from above:

  • प्रत्ययः (3.1.1) — a suffix (governing up to chapter 5)
  • परश्च (3.1.2) — and it comes after (governing up to chapter 5)
  • धातोः (3.1.91) — after the verbal root (governing up to chapter 3)

Arranging all words in connected order (अन्वय) produces the वृत्ति:

वर्तमाने धातोः परः लट् प्रत्ययः (भवति) "In the present tense, the suffix लट् comes after the verbal root."

2.2 Deriving the वृत्ति Using अनुवृत्ति (Carried-Over Words)

Consider हलन्त्यम् (1.3.3).

पदच्छेद: हल् १/१, अन्त्यम् १/१ — a two-word sutra. From the sutra above it, उपदेशेऽजनुनासिक इत् (1.3.2), two words carry over (अनुवृत्ति): 'उपदेशे' and 'इत्'. The वृत्ति becomes:

उपदेशे अन्त्यं हल् इत् (भवति)

"In the context of उपदेश (the original enunciation of a grammatical form), the final consonant is designated it (a marker)."

आद्योच्चारणम् (original enunciation) - 1. अष्टाध्यायी सूत्रपाठ 2. धातुपाठ 3. गणपाठ 4. उणादिकोष 5. लिङ्गानुशासनम् 6. शिक्षा (Sometimes stated explicitly)

Similarly for न विभक्तौ तुस्माः (1.3.4). पदच्छेद: न — an indeclinable (avyaya); विभक्तौ ७/१; तुस्माः १/३. Only one word carries over from 1.3.2 — namely 'इत्'. See the economy of words, he did not bring अन्त्यं हल् from 1.3.3, just न clearly stated it is negating rule directly above. The वृत्ति becomes:

विभक्तौ तुस्माः इत् न (भवन्ति)

"In a vibhakti (case or personal ending), the members of the tu-class (त-series), स् and म् are not designated it."*

In this way, a student can derive the meaning of every sutra — with a smile.

3. The Rule Governing अनुवृत्ति

अनुवृत्ति (the carry-over of words from prior sutras into later ones) follows a precise principle:

A word standing in a particular विभक्ति (grammatical case) in the current sutra will not be carried over from a prior sutra in that same विभक्ति.

However, an adjective (विशेषण) or predicate in the same विभक्ति may still come.

Example: In हलन्त्यम् (1.3.3), both 'हल्' (noun) and 'अन्त्यम्' (adjective) are in the first case (prathama). Therefore, from उपदेशेऽजनुनासिक इत् (1.3.2), the subject-adjective pair 'अच् अनुनासिकः' — also first case — does not carry over. But 'उपदेशे' (seventh case) and 'इत्' (predicate) both do.

In न विभक्तौ तुस्माः (1.3.4), 'विभक्तौ' is already in the seventh case, so 'उपदेशे' (also seventh case) cannot come. And 'तुस्माः' is the first-case subject, so no first-case subject from the sutra above can carry over either.

The key principle: the syntactic need (आकाङ्क्षा) of the later sutra is the principal factor governing which words of prior sutras carry forward.

(Students who grasp this will not only be able to derive the सूत्रार्थ on their own, but will develop keen analytical intelligence — a gift that Panini built into the very structure of his grammar.)

3.1 Rule 1 — No Same-Vibhakti Carry-Over

A word in a given विभक्ति within a sutra does not carry over from the sutra above in that same विभक्ति.

Example: ग्क्ङिति च (1.1.5) contains the seventh-case word 'ङिति'. Therefore, from न धातुलोप आर्धधातुके (1.1.4), only 'न' carries over, and the entire sutra इको गुणवृद्धी (1.1.3) also comes in. The वृत्ति becomes:

ङिति च न इको गुणवृद्धी (भवतः)

Note: Wherever no explicit verb is present, 'अस्ति' or 'भवति' is simply supplied — this is universally understood.

3.2 Rule 2 — The Sixth Case and sthāne

Wherever a sixth-case (ṣaṣṭhī) word appears without a specific relational sense, the word 'स्थाने' ("in the place of") is added after it to construct the वृत्ति. Panini himself wrote the paribhasha षष्ठी स्थानेयोगा (1.1.49) to codify this.

Example: In इको यर्णाचि (6.1.77), the word 'इकः' is in the sixth case without specific relational meaning. We add 'स्थाने': 'इकः स्थाने'. With the adhikara from संहितायाम् (6.1.72) carrying forward:

इकः स्थाने यण् भवति अचि परे संहितायाम्

"In saṃhitā, in the place of ik, the substitute yaṇ occurs when a vowel follows."

The very next sutra, एचोऽयवायावः (6.1.78), has its own sixth-case and first-case words, so 'इकः' and 'यण्' do not carry over. Only 'अचि' (seventh case) carries forward. For 'एचः' (sixth case here), 'स्थाने' is added. The वृत्ति becomes:

एचः स्थाने अयवायावः भवन्ति अचि संहितायाम्

"In saṃhitā, in the place of ec, the substitutes ay, av, āy, āv occur when a vowel follows."

In this way, the वृत्ति of such sutras constructs itself naturally.

4. Implicit Words That Arrive Automatically

Certain technical words in the Ashtadhyayi, when they appear in or are carried into a sutra, automatically bring additional sixth-case words with them. Knowing these conventions makes वृत्ति-construction effortless.

4.1 When guṇa or vṛddhi Appears

Wherever 'गुणः' or 'वृद्धिः' appears — in the sutra itself or carried over from above — the sixth-case word 'इकः' arrives automatically, governed by इको गुणवृद्धी (1.1.3).

Example: In सार्वधातुकार्द्धधातुकयोः (7.3.84), the अधिकार अङ्गस्य (6.4.1) is in force, and 'गुणः' comes from मिदेर्गुणः (7.3.82). When 'गुणः' arrives, 'इकः' is automatically supplied. Then, by येन विधिस्तदन्तस्य (1.1.72), 'अन्त' is appended to the adjective 'इकः', giving 'इगन्तस्य'. The वृत्ति becomes:

इगन्तस्य अङ्गस्य गुणः भवति सार्वधातुकार्द्धधातुकयोः परयोः

4.2 When hrasva, dīrgha, or pluta Appears

Wherever 'ह्रस्व' (short), 'दीर्घ' (long), or 'प्लुत' (protracted) appears — whether in the sutra or carried over — the sixth-case word 'अचः' is automatically supplied, governed by ऊकालोऽज्झस्वदीर्घप्लुतः (1.2.27).

Example: ह्रस्वः (7.4.59) prescribes shortening. From the sutra above, अत्र लोपोऽभ्यासस्य (7.4.58), 'अभ्यासस्य' carries over. Because of the word 'ह्रस्व', 'अचः' is automatically supplied. The वृत्ति becomes:

अभ्यासस्य अचः ह्रस्वः भवति

"The vowel of the abhyāsa (reduplication syllable) becomes short."

5. The अन्त and आदि Conventions

Two further conventions help expand adjectives in the वृत्ति:

  • For adjectives in cases other than the seventh (e.g. fifth, sixth): append 'अन्त' and keep the same vibhakti. Governed by येन विधिस्तदन्तस्य (1.1.72).
  • For seventh-case adjectives: append 'आदि' and retain the seventh-case form. Governed by यस्मिन् विधिस्तदादावल्ग्रहणे (paribhasha).

Example: In सुपि (7.3.102), with the adhikara अङ्गस्य (6.4.1) and the carried-over sutra अतो दीर्घो यञि (7.3.101), we have 'अतः' (sixth-case adjective) and 'यञि' (seventh-case). Applying the conventions:

  • 'अत्' + अन्त → 'अदन्तस्य' (sixth case retained)
  • 'यञ्' + आदि → 'यञादौ' (seventh case retained)

The वृत्ति becomes:

अदन्तस्य अङ्गस्य यञादौ सुपि दीर्घः (भवति)

"The aṅga ending in a takes a long vowel before a sup-suffix beginning with yaṇ."*

Similarly, in नामि (6.4.3), 'दीर्घः' appears, automatically supplying 'अचः'. With the adhikara अङ्गस्य (6.4.1) in force and 'अन्त' appended, the वृत्ति becomes:

अजन्तस्य अङ्गस्य दीर्घः भवति नामि परे

"The aṅga ending in a vowel takes a long vowel when nāmin follows."

6. Priority Rules: para, nitya, antaraṅga, apavāda

When two rules could both apply at the same point in a derivation, the Ashtadhyayi has a hierarchy to determine which wins. Knowledge of this hierarchy is essential.

Priority Rule type Meaning
1st (weakest) पर The later sutra (by order in the text)
2nd नित्य An obligatory rule — applies whether or not a prior operation took place
3rd अन्तरङ्ग An internally motivated rule — requires fewer external conditions
4th (strongest) अपवाद An exception — has no scope of its own except to override the general rule

The governing sutra is विप्रतिषेधे परं कार्यम् (1.4.2) — when two rules conflict, the later one applies. But this is the weakest of the four: नित्य beats पर, अन्तरङ्ग beats पर, and अपवाद beats all three.

परनित्यान्तरङ्गापवादानामुत्तरोत्तरं बलीयः "Of para, nitya, antaraṅga, and apavāda — each successive one is stronger than the last."

7. The Rule pūrvatrāsiddham (8.2.1)

पूर्वत्रासिद्धम् (8.2.1) opens the second पाद of the eighth chapter. It declares:

The sutras of the त्रिपादि (the last three पाद of the Ashtadhyayi — 8.2, 8.3, 8.4) are treated as non-existent (असिद्ध, suspended) with respect to all preceding sutras (chapters 1–8.1).

This means: when a त्रिपादि rule has already applied in a derivation, earlier rules cannot "see" its effect — they must operate as if it had not happened.

When पूर्वत्रासिद्धम् itself becomes an अधिकार rule, it also governs within the त्रिपादि: later sutras within 8.2–8.4 are असिद्ध with respect to earlier ones there too.

Example 1: हरे + इह

  1. एचोऽयवायावः (6.1.78): 'ए' → 'अय्' → हय् + इह
  2. लोपः शाकल्यस्य (8.3.19): 'य्' deleted → हर + इह
  3. आद् गुणः (6.1.87) would now seem to apply (अ + इ → ए).
  4. But पूर्वत्रासिद्धम् (8.2.1): लोपः शाकल्यस्य (8.3.19) is असिद्ध from the perspective of आद् गुणः (6.1.87). The deletion never happened — 'य्' is still present. The sequence is अ + यि, not अ + इ.
  5. Therefore आद् गुणः (6.1.87) does not apply, and the correct form is preserved.

Example 2: सत् + चित्

  1. झलां जशोऽन्ते (8.2.19) renders स्तोः शचुना श्चुः (8.4.40) असिद्ध and changes 'त्' → 'द्'
  2. स्तोः शचुना श्चुः (8.4.40): 'त्' → 'च्'
  3. खरि च (8.4.55): 'च्' → 'क्'
  4. Result: सच्चित्

8. Closing Words

All of the above is knowledge that every student of Paninian grammar must acquire. Most essential of all is reading the sutras in order. In sequential reading alone lie simplicity, sharpening of the intellect, and countless other benefits that cannot be put into words.

This, indeed, is the science of the Ashtadhyayi.

Footnote on the priority definitions:

कृताकृतप्रसङ्ग विधिनित्यः । अल्पापेक्षकमन्तरङ्गम् । बह्वपेक्षकं बहिरङ्गम् । निरवकाशो विधिरपवादः । प्राप्तौ सत्यां विधिर्नियमः स्यात् ।

A rule that applies whether or not a prior operation has taken place is nitya (obligatory). A rule requiring fewer auxiliary conditions is antaraṅga (internally motivated); one requiring more is bahiraṅga (externally motivated). A rule that has no scope of its own is an apavāda (exception). A rule that applies only when the result is already otherwise obtainable is a niyama (restrictive rule).
